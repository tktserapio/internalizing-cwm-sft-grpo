## Prisoner's Dilemma Game Rules

The Prisoner's Dilemma is a classic game theory scenario that demonstrates why two rational individuals might not cooperate, even when it appears in their best interest to do so.

### 1. Setup & Preliminaries
- **Players:** 2 (Player 0 and Player 1)
- **Actions:** Each player secretly chooses either "cooperate" or "defect"
- **Simultaneous Play:** Both players reveal their choices at the same time

### 2. Game Flow
1. **Player 0** secretly commits to either "C" (cooperate) or "D" (defect)
2. **Player 1** secretly commits to either "C" or "D" (without seeing P0's choice)
3. **Reveal:** Both choices are revealed simultaneously and payoffs are computed

### 3. Payoff Matrix
The payoffs are based on the classic Prisoner's Dilemma structure:

|           | P1: C      | P1: D      |
|-----------|------------|------------|
| P0: C     | (3, 3)     | (0, 5)     |
| P0: D     | (5, 0)     | (1, 1)     |

- **Mutual Cooperation (C, C):** Both get 3 (reward for cooperation)
- **Mutual Defection (D, D):** Both get 1 (punishment for mutual defection)
- **Temptation (D vs C):** Defector gets 5, cooperator gets 0

### 4. Strategic Notes
- The dominant strategy for each player is to defect
- However, mutual cooperation yields a better outcome than mutual defection
- This creates the fundamental dilemma: individual rationality leads to a socially suboptimal outcome
- The Nash equilibrium is (D, D) with payoffs (1, 1)

### 5. Move Notation
- **C** for Cooperate
- **D** for Defect

## Action String Specification
Player actions: ["C", "D"]
  - "C": Cooperate
  - "D": Defect
